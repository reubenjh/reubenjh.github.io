Can you think of an analogy to describe HTML, CSS and the DOM? How would you describe it to your non-technical friend?

There seems to be one most common analogy to describe the relationship here so for the sake of ease I'd use that - building a house. The HTML is your architect's blueprints, and defines the basic structure. It's your walls, your roof, where the doors go, what rooms there are etc. The CSS is the designer, and defines how things look. Your colours, features, making sure the feng shui is awesome. The DOM, as far as I can tell, is the construction company's best interpretation of how all your collective blueprints and design rules fit together to create a finished product, represented in one visual document.

A cooler analogy though would be to look at HTML as the big lego monster in the image above. It's ugly and a bit dumb (sorry monster dude) but it's powerful. It get's dragged around and told where to go by the Stormtrooper, who is like the CSS. The DOM is created in the mind of the little Ewoks whose village these Stormtroopers and monster are gonna stomp. It's the picture they put together of what's going on and what will happen? I'm stretching a bit here, never mind.

In the world of websites, (I think) technically a browser arriving at your webpage sees your HTML and CSS and creates a DOM for the page for it to display. It might look identical to the HTML you wrote but it might not. Different browsers may interpret your code slightly differently to display slightly different things, or they may correct obvious errors such as a missing tbody element within your table, and simply insert the tbody

What is meant by boxifying design?

To boxify a design is to draw boxes around all of the distinct objects and elements in a design to ascertain their relative position and relationship to one another - i.e. which boxes are within other boxes.

What is the box model?

The box model is a CSS design and layout model that considers all HTML elements "boxes", consisting of 4 layers: "content", "padding", "border" and "margin".


This allows us to define the size of any html element and the space between.
